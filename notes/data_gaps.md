# Data Gaps & What These Numbers Cannot See

## What the Shape of the FBI Data Reveals

The gaps and discontinuities in the FBI dataset are not incidental — they are structural, and they mean something.

- **1991:** The Hate Crime Statistics Act passed in 1990. Collection began in 1992. The first year the law existed, no one counted. The mandate preceded the mechanism.
- **1993–1999:** The only confirmed figure in this stretch is 1993 (258) and 1995 (355). Four years — 1994, 1996, 1997, 1998, 1999 — have no recoverable annual figure in public records. This is not because nothing happened. It is because no one assembled it into a number that survived.
- **2013:** The OMB mandated separating "Asian" from "Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander." The apparent drop in incidents after this year may not reflect any real change — it may be that Pacific Islander victims were simply reclassified out of the count. A bureaucratic redefinition that looks like improvement.
- **2021:** The year of the largest recorded spike (746 incidents, +168%) is also the year the FBI transitioned to NIBRS, dropping ~3,300 agencies from participation. The peak is real. The number is also the least comparably measured in the entire dataset. The most visible moment of violence is the hardest to precisely quantify.

These are not data quality problems to be corrected. They are the record of how the state has — and has not — chosen to see.

## The Fundamental Problem: Both Datasets Are Floors, Not Ceilings

Every number in this dataset is an undercount. The question is only by how much.

---

## FBI Gaps

### 1. Non-reporting
The Bureau of Justice Statistics (NCVS) estimates that only ~54% of violent hate crimes are reported to police at all. Fear of police, immigration status, language barriers, distrust, and shame all suppress reporting. Among AAPI communities — with significant immigrant populations and historical reason to distrust law enforcement — this suppression may be even greater.

### 2. Non-classification
Of crimes that ARE reported, only those where an officer determines "sufficient evidence of bias motivation" get classified as hate crimes. Bias is notoriously hard to prove. Many attacks on Asian Americans are logged as ordinary assault, robbery, or vandalism without the hate crime enhancement.

### 3. Non-submission
Of agencies that participate in UCR/NIBRS, participation quality varies. Some agencies submit no hate crimes year after year — not because none occurred, but because their officers are not trained to classify them. The 2021 NIBRS transition reduced participating agencies by ~3,300, creating a discontinuity in the data.

### 4. Scope (criminal only)
FBI captures zero non-criminal acts: no verbal harassment, no deliberate avoidance, no discrimination in services, no online hate. These are often the most frequent forms of bias experienced.

### 5. Historical pre-2010 data
Pre-2010 FBI hate crime data is less reliable due to lower agency participation rates. Long-term trend analysis is difficult.

## What the Shape of the STOP AAPI Hate Data Reveals

### The retroactive reclassification (November 2023)

In November 2023, STOP AAPI Hate published a new hate acts taxonomy and ran all 2020–2021 incidents through it retroactively. The framework sorted reports into three tiers:
- **Explicit bias** — direct racial slurs, unambiguous statements of animus
- **Coded bias** — language that implies race without naming it ("go back to China," COVID-related targeting)
- **Perceived bias** — reporter believes the act was racially motivated, but evidence is ambiguous

Incidents that fell into the ambiguous tier were removed from the official totals. The result:

| Year | Original count | Revised count | Removed |
|------|---------------|---------------|---------|
| 2020 | 4,632 | 4,409 | 223 |
| 2021 | 6,273 | 5,679 | 594 |

The revision was an act of methodological rigor. It was also a retroactive reduction of the crisis — the peak, as first reported in real time during the height of the violence, was quietly walked back three years later. The people who submitted those 817 reports did not change their experience. The framework that decided whether their experience counted changed around them.

This is not a criticism of STOP AAPI Hate. It is a description of what categorization does. Every act of counting involves a decision about what counts. That decision can be revised. The revision is part of the data.

### The 2022–2024 shift to survey methodology

After 2021, STOP AAPI Hate largely stopped publishing annual incident counts and pivoted to population surveys conducted with NORC at the University of Chicago. The incident counts that do exist for 2022 and 2023 (1,321 and 735) are dramatically lower than 2021 — but the surveys show hate *experience* remaining high: 49% of AAPI adults in 2023, 53% in 2024.

STOP AAPI Hate has not published a full explanation for the drop. Possible factors include platform saturation (the community most likely to report had already reported), organizational bandwidth consumed by the taxonomy reclassification project, and a deliberate strategic shift. The 2024 incident count has not been released at all as of March 2026.

What this means: the years when the organization chose to stop publishing granular counts are the same years we have the least visibility into. Whether the incidents declined, plateaued, or continued at pace — the data does not say. The surveys suggest the latter.

### The 2024 gap

The cumulative total of 12,803 (2020–2024) implies approximately 659 incidents in 2024 by arithmetic subtraction. But STOP AAPI Hate has not published this figure. It is derived, not reported. It appears in this dataset as an empty cell, not a number, because a calculation is not the same as a count.

## STOP AAPI Hate Gaps

### 1. Platform awareness
You can only report if you know the platform exists. The reporting center launched March 19, 2020 and grew through media coverage. Early months likely undercounted; rural and less-connected communities remain underrepresented.

### 2. Self-selection
People who report to STOP AAPI Hate are self-selecting: likely more politically engaged, more English/digitally proficient (though multilingual forms exist), more willing to name what happened as hate. Incidents experienced by isolated or elderly AAPI individuals may be systematically missed.

### 3. No verification
Reports are not investigated or verified. There is potential for double-counting (same incident reported by multiple people) and for inaccurate classification by reporters.

### 4. Non-criminal framing
STOP AAPI Hate's broader definition (which captures everyday discrimination) means its numbers are not directly comparable to FBI crime statistics. The 12,803 vs. ~407 gap does NOT mean STOP AAPI Hate captures 30x more crimes — it's measuring different things.

### 5. Recency bias
Incidents may be reported with delay. Trauma, shame, or uncertainty about whether something "counts" can delay or prevent reporting.

## What Neither Dataset Captures

- **Structural / institutional racism:** Redlining legacy, model minority myth weaponization, employment discrimination, school bullying, medical mistreatment
- **Micro-aggressions:** Daily "where are you really from," assumptions about foreignness, being spoken to slowly
- **Internalized harm:** AAPI individuals who don't name their experiences as hate
- **Incidents that kill slowly:** Psychological trauma, anxiety, self-isolation, economic loss from fear of public space

---

## The Number That Doesn't Exist

There is no definitive count of anti-Asian hate in America. There is:
- The number the state acknowledges (FBI)
- The number the community reported to one platform (STOP AAPI Hate)
- Survey estimates of experience (NORC: 53% of AAPI adults in 2024)
- The actual number: unknown, and perhaps unknowable

That gap — between what is counted and what is lived — is the subject of this project.
